Methodology

This project approaches feng shui not as a system of belief, but as a spatial and cultural framework for interpreting architecture.

Analytical Position

Feng shui is treated here as a historically situated way of thinking about orientation, movement, balance, and the relationship between built form and environment. Rather than assuming its universal validity, I examine how its principles align, conflict, or adapt when applied across different cultural and religious contexts.

Method

Each case study follows a consistent analytical process:

  • Observation of site orientation and surrounding geography
  • Analysis of circulation, thresholds, and spatial hierarchy
  • Consideration of historical and cultural context
  • Interpretation through selected feng shui concepts as analytical tools

Cross-Cultural Application

When examining non-Chinese religious architecture, feng shui is not imposed as an evaluative standard. Instead, it functions as a comparative lens, allowing differences in spatial logic and symbolic emphasis to become visible. This approach prioritizes respect for internal religious meanings while exploring points of resonance and divergence.

Limitations

This methodology does not claim predictive or normative authority. Its purpose is interpretive: to understand how ideas about space, order, and meaning are embedded within architectural form and shaped by cultural context.
